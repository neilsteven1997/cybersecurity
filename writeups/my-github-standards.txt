# TryHackMe - Advent of Cyber 2025 - Day 14: Containers Doordashers Demise

**Date Completed**: 16 December 2025  
**Platform**: TryHackMe - Advent of Cyber 2025  
**Difficulty**: Medium  
**Time Spent**: 2.5 hours  
**Key Topics**: Docker Containers, Privilege Escalation, Container Breakout  

## Objective
The goal of this room was to identify misconfigured Docker containers, enumerate running services, and achieve privilege escalation to root via container escape techniques.

## Tools Used
- nmap
- docker CLI
- curl
- linpeas / pspy (for enumeration)

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Initial Enumeration**  
   Performed a full port scan on the target machine:  


![Nmap scan results showing open ports](images/aoc-2025/day-14-nmap-scan.png)

2. **Identifying Docker Socket Exposure**  
Discovered the Docker socket was accessible via a web service.  
Used curl to list running containers:  


![Docker containers list output](images/aoc-2025/day-14-docker-list.png)

3. **Container Escape**  
Entered the vulnerable container and mounted the host filesystem to gain root access.

![Successful root shell after container breakout](images/aoc-2025/day-14-root-flag.png)

## Key Takeaways
- Always restrict Docker socket exposure in production.
- Misconfigured privileges can lead to full host compromise.
- Regular auditing of running containers is essential.

For the **Recommendations** section in your cybersecurity audit report (e.g., for Botium Toys, as described: "provide recommendations, related to controls and/or compliance needs, that your IT manager could communicate to stakeholders to reduce risks to assets and improve Botium Toys’ security posture"), here's a clean, professional, and commonly used format when you have multiple recommendations (like your example of 3).

This structure draws from standard practices in infosec audit reports — it's scannable for stakeholders, ties directly to risks/controls/compliance, and emphasizes actionability.

### Overall Section Layout (when you have 3+ recommendations)

**Recommendations**

The audit identified several opportunities to strengthen Botium Toys' security controls, reduce exposure to cyber risks (e.g., data breaches, ransomware, unauthorized access), and better align with relevant standards (NIST CSF, ISO 27001, SOC 2, etc.). The recommendations below are prioritized by risk level and potential impact. Each includes a clear action, rationale, priority/timeline, and suggested owner to facilitate communication to leadership and implementation tracking.

Recommendations are listed in **priority order** (Critical first, then High, Medium, Low).

**Recommendation 1** (or **REC-01** for tracking)  
**Title/Brief Issue**  
(e.g., Implement Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) for Privileged Accounts)  

**Related Finding**  
Brief link to the observation (1 sentence):  
No MFA is enforced on administrative or remote access accounts, leaving them vulnerable to credential theft and unauthorized access.

**Risk Level & Impact**  
Critical / High / Medium / Low  
(e.g., **High** – Credential compromise could lead to full network access, data exfiltration of customer PII, ransomware deployment, and potential regulatory penalties under CCPA or similar.)

**Compliance / Control Reference**  
(e.g., NIST CSF PR.AC-7, SOC 2 CC6.1, ISO 27001 A.9.4.2)

**Recommended Action**  
Specific, imperative steps:  
- Deploy phishing-resistant MFA (e.g., authenticator apps, hardware tokens, or FIDO2) for all privileged/administrative accounts, VPN, email, and cloud consoles.  
- Begin rollout with executive/IT admin accounts within the first phase.  
- Enforce via policy and technical controls (e.g., conditional access policies in Microsoft Entra ID or equivalent).  
- Provide user awareness training during rollout.

**Priority & Target Timeline**  
High – Complete implementation within 90 days.

**Owner / Responsible Party**  
IT Security Lead / CISO (with support from IT Operations)

**Expected Benefit**  
Reduces successful account takeover attacks by over 90% (industry benchmark) and demonstrates proactive compliance alignment.

---

**Recommendation 2** (REC-02)  
**Title/Brief Issue**  
Establish Formal Patch Management Process

**Related Finding**  
Multiple systems are running outdated software with known vulnerabilities and no consistent patching cadence.

**Risk Level & Impact**  
**Critical** – Known exploits could enable ransomware or remote code execution, leading to business disruption and data loss.

**Compliance / Control Reference**  
NIST PR.IP-12, ISO 27001 A.12.6.1

**Recommended Action**  
- Document and implement a patch management policy.  
- Perform monthly automated vulnerability scanning.  
- Prioritize patching: critical within 7 days, high within 30 days.  
- Test patches in a staging environment; use compensating controls (e.g., segmentation) for legacy systems.

**Priority & Target Timeline**  
Critical – Scanning in place within 30 days; full process within 120 days.

**Owner / Responsible Party**  
IT Operations Manager

**Expected Benefit**  
Significantly lowers exploit risk from known vulnerabilities and supports audit/compliance evidence.

---

**Recommendation 3** (REC-03)  
**Title/Brief Issue**  
Conduct Regular Security Awareness Training and Phishing Simulations

**Related Finding**  
Employees lack ongoing training, increasing susceptibility to social engineering attacks.

**Risk Level & Impact**  
**Medium** – Phishing remains a top initial access vector; successful attacks could escalate to credential compromise or malware.

**Compliance / Control Reference**  
NIST AT-2, SOC 2 CC1.4, ISO 27001 A.7.2.2

**Recommended Action**  
- Roll out mandatory annual security awareness training plus quarterly phishing simulations.  
- Track completion rates (>95% target) and simulation click/fail rates.  
- Target high-risk roles (e.g., finance, execs) with additional tailored content.

**Priority & Target Timeline**  
Medium – First training cycle within 60 days; ongoing program within 6 months.

**Owner / Responsible Party**  
HR / IT Security (joint)

**Expected Benefit**  
Reduces human-error-based incidents and builds a security-aware culture.

### Additional Tips 
- **Start with a short intro paragraph** (as shown above) to set context.
- **Order by priority** — stakeholders focus on Critical/High first.
- **Keep each recommendation to 1/2 page max** — concise but complete.
- **Use consistent headings** (bold them) so it's easy to scan.
- **End the section** optionally with: "The IT manager should prioritize these in a remediation roadmap, assign resources, and report progress quarterly to leadership."

- **Audience & scrutiny**: In strict environments, reports may be read by boards, regulators, external auditors, legal teams, or become part of compliance evidence. Anything that sounds too informal could be questioned or weaken the document's defensibility.
- **Best practices from sources**: Industry guidance (e.g., AuditBoard, RSI Security, IIA standards, TrustedSec) stresses:
  
  - Clarity and plain language → yes (short sentences, avoid heavy jargon).
  - Business impact focus → yes (link tech issues to money/reputation/fines/downtime).
  - But **professional tone** — objective, no colloquialisms, no emotional language.
  - Executive summaries/exec-facing sections should be simplified for non-tech readers, but still polished.

### Recommended Approach for Strict/Formal Environments
Use a **balanced, executive-friendly style** that's concise and easy to grasp — but keeps a professional voice. This is what most mature orgs do:

**Recommendations – Prioritized Actions to Strengthen Security**

The audit identified three key gaps that increase the risk of data breaches, ransomware, regulatory penalties, or operational disruption. Addressing these will materially improve Botium Toys' security posture and compliance alignment.

**1. Enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on Privileged Accounts**  
Current state: Administrative and remote-access accounts rely solely on passwords, making them vulnerable to credential theft.  
Business impact: Unauthorized access could compromise customer data or critical systems.  
Action: Implement phishing-resistant MFA (e.g., authenticator apps or hardware tokens) for all privileged, administrative, and remote/cloud accounts, starting with executive and IT users.  
Timeline: Complete within 90 days.  
Owner: IT Security Lead.  
Benefit: Significantly reduces credential-based attack success rates.

**2. Implement a Formal Patch Management Program**  
Current state: Inconsistent patching leaves systems exposed to known vulnerabilities.  
Business impact: Exploitation could cause extended downtime or data loss.  
Action: Establish monthly vulnerability scanning, prioritized patching (critical within 7 days, high within 30 days), and testing/segmentation for legacy systems.  
Timeline: Scanning operational within 30 days; full program within 120 days.  
Owner: IT Operations Manager.  
Benefit: Closes known exploit paths and supports compliance evidence.

**3. Establish Ongoing Security Awareness Training**  
Current state: Limited employee training increases phishing susceptibility.  
Business impact: Social engineering remains a primary attack vector.  
Action: Deliver mandatory annual training and quarterly phishing simulations, with tracking of completion and performance metrics.  
Timeline: Initial cycle within 60 days; ongoing program.  
Owner: IT Security (with HR support).  
Benefit: Reduces human-error incidents and strengthens overall resilience.

**Next Steps**  
Prioritize implementation in this order. The IT manager will prepare a remediation plan with resource needs and provide quarterly progress updates to leadership.

This version is still **short, scannable, business-focused**, and very easy for non-technical stakeholders to understand — but it uses professional phrasing suitable for formal reports, board decks, or compliance submissions. It's the sweet spot used in most real-world cybersecurity audit communications to executives.
