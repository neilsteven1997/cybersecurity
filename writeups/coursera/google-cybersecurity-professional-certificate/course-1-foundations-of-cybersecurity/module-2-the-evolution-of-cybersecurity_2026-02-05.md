# Module 2: The Evolution Of Cybersecurity

---

In my ongoing notes on cybersecurity fundamentals, I've been reflecting on how historical incidents have driven the field's development,
particularly through early malware like the Brain virus from 1986, crafted by the Alvi brothers to monitor unauthorized software copies
but inadvertently spreading worldwide via disks, disrupting operations without data destruction. That event underscored the urgency for 
robust security protocols to balance protection and efficiency. Similarly, the 1988 Morris worm, intended by Robert Morris to gauge 
internet scale, spiraled out of control by reinstalling repeatedly on untracked machines, crippling around 6,000 systems and incurring 
massive costs, which prompted the creation of Computer Emergency Response Teams to handle such crises systematically. These precursors 
echo in later threats like the LoveLetter or ILOVEYOU virus, reinforcing the need for incident response frameworks now known as computer
security incident response teams.

Diving deeper into prevalent tactics, phishing stands out as a deceptive use of electronic messaging to extract confidential details or 
install harmful code, often manifesting as business email compromise where imposters mimic trusted contacts for monetary gain, or spear
phishing aimed at particular individuals from seemingly reliable origins. Whaling escalates this by zeroing in on high-level executives
for sensitive intelligence, while vishing leverages voice calls and smishing exploits texts to impersonate authorities and harvest data. 
Malware, broadly software engineered to impair hardware or connections, pursues financial or strategic edges, with viruses requiring 
user activation through tainted attachments to embed and corrupt files, worms autonomously propagating across linked devices, ransomware
locking information until ransoms are paid, and spyware covertly amassing and monetizing personal communications, recordings, and 
positions.

Social engineering emerges as a core vulnerability exploitation, preying on unchecked trust to secure restricted access or assets, with 
variants like gathering victim profiles from online platforms to launch tailored strikes, compromising popular sites in watering hole 
scenarios to infect niche audiences, dropping infected USB drives for unwitting installation, or physically posing as insiders to 
breach facilities. Its potency stems from human tendencies toward deference and haste, amplified by expanding social networks that 
expose personal details, making data sharing a double-edged convenience. I find it striking how these methods thrive on psychological 
levers, turning everyday interactions into risks.

Expanding on attacker profiles, advanced persistent threats represent sophisticated intruders who meticulously scout large entities or 
governments to linger undetected, aiming to sabotage vital systems or pilfer proprietary innovations. Insider threats, conversely, 
misuse granted privileges for sabotage, graft, spying, or leaks, posing internal dangers that demand vigilant oversight. Hacktivists, 
fueled by ideological drives, harness tech for protests, messaging, or notoriety, while hackers span a spectrum: ethical ones legally 
probe defenses to fortify them, semi-authorized researchers uncover flaws without exploitation, and unethical variants traffic in 
stolen data for profit. Novice actors often experiment to hone abilities, exact retribution, or leverage ready-made tools, whereas 
contract-based ones tackle diverse assignments, and some position themselves as guardians against malice. This diversity in motives 
sharpens my approach to tailoring defenses accordingly.

Further attack vectors include password assaults seeking to crack secured entry points via exhaustive trials or precomputed tables, 
aligning with network safeguards; broader social manipulations as noted; physical intrusions impacting tangible spaces through rigged 
cables, drives, or card duplication, tied to asset protection; adversarial artificial intelligence twisting machine learning for 
streamlined offensives, bridging network and access controls; supply-chain compromises infiltrating production chains to embed malware,
spanning risk oversight, design, and operations; and cryptographic disruptions undermining encrypted exchanges through probability 
exploits or version downgrades, focused on communication integrity. These incidents, varying in complexity, map to the eight Certified
Information Systems Security Professional domains, which frame professional responsibilities.

The security and risk management domain centers on outlining protective aims, alleviating dangers, upholding legal standards, and 
ensuring operational resilience, such as revising guidelines for compliance with rules like HIPAA. Asset security guards tangible and
intangible holdings through their lifecycle, including safe erasure of outdated gear and records. Security architecture and engineering
prioritizes fortifying data via refined mechanisms, like firewall setups to thwart intrusions while sustaining workflow. Communication
and network security oversees wired and wireless links, crafting rules to counter hazards from open connections. Identity and access 
management enforces policy adherence in regulating physical and digital resources, verifying identities and logging permissions to 
restrict sensitive info access. Security assessment and testing entails evaluating controls, scrutinizing data, and auditing for 
exposures, routinely reviewing user rights. Security operations handle probes and proactive defenses, such as addressing rogue network
devices per protocol. Software development security integrates safe programming from inception, advising on features like credential
handling in apps.

During breaches, composure is vital for incident leads to navigate panic, prioritizing halts to exfiltration—potentially isolating 
systems—before probes, guided by predefined plans that entry-level roles must master. This structured mindset has proven invaluable 
in my simulations, preventing escalation.

For precise terminology, the National Institute of Standards and Technology glossary offers comprehensive explanations, and I've found
the Hacking Google video series insightful for team dynamics in threat mitigation.

### Key Takeaways
- Early attacks shaped response strategies, including the Brain virus (1986) spreading via disks to track copies but slowing
  productivity, and the Morris worm (1988) crashing systems by relentless replication, leading to CERT formation.
- Phishing variants: Business email compromise for financial scams via forged requests; spear phishing targeting specifics from trusted
   facades; whaling against executives for data; vishing via voice impersonation; smishing through deceptive texts.
- Malware types: Viruses activated by users to corrupt via attachments; worms self-spreading network-wide; ransomware encrypting for
   extortion; spyware stealing communications and locations.
- Social engineering methods: Profiling from social media for attacks; watering hole compromises of frequented sites; USB baiting with
   planted infected drives; physical impersonation for entry.
- Effectiveness principles: Authority via power mimicry; intimidation through coercion; consensus by citing group norms; scarcity
  implying limits; familiarity forging bonds; trust built over time; urgency demanding swift action.
- Additional attacks: Password via brute force or rainbow tables; physical with malicious cables, drives, or skimmers; adversarial AI
  manipulating learning systems; supply-chain exploiting vendor weaknesses; cryptographic like birthday, collision, or downgrade.
- Threat actors: Advanced persistent threats for long-term infrastructure or IP theft; insiders for internal harm; hacktivists for
  political aims; ethical hackers for legal testing; semi-authorized for vulnerability spotting; unethical for illicit gains; novices
  for skill-building or revenge.
- CISSP domains: Security and risk management for goals, risks, compliance, continuity; asset security for asset lifecycle protection;
  security architecture and engineering for tool optimization; communication and network security for connection management; identity
  and access management for policy enforcement and access control; security assessment and testing for audits and monitoring; security
   operations for investigations and prevention; software development security for secure coding.
- Breach response: Maintain calm for decisions; contain loss first; follow incident plans to investigate post-stoppage.
